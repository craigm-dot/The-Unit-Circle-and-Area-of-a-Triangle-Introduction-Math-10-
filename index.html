Learning Objectives: The Unit Circle

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define the unit circle and understand its equation \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\)
  • Understand how angles are measured on the unit circle (positive for anticlockwise, negative for clockwise)
  • Define sine and cosine using the unit circle for any angle
  • Recognize that \(-1 \leq \cos\theta \leq 1\) and \(-1 \leq \sin\theta \leq 1\)
  • Apply the Pythagorean identity: \(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\)
  • Use supplementary angle relationships:
    • \(\cos(180° - \theta) = -\cos\theta\)
    • \(\sin(180° - \theta) = \sin\theta\)

What is the Unit Circle?

Definition: The unit circle is the circle with centre \((0, 0)\) and radius 1 unit.

Equation of the Unit Circle:

\[x^2 + y^2 = 1\]

This comes from the distance formula: any point \((x, y)\) on the circle is exactly 1 unit from the origin.

Angle Measurement on the Unit Circle

Angles on the unit circle are measured from the positive x-axis.

Convention for Angle Direction:

  • Positive angles: measured anticlockwise (counterclockwise)
  • Negative angles: measured clockwise

Definition of Sine and Cosine

In Chapter 7, we learned trigonometric ratios for acute angles in right triangles. Now we extend these definitions to any angle using the unit circle.

Definition Using Right Triangle Trigonometry:

For a point \(P(a, b)\) on the unit circle in the first quadrant:

\[\cos\theta = \frac{\text{ADJ}}{\text{HYP}} = \frac{a}{1} = a\]

\[\sin\theta = \frac{\text{OPP}}{\text{HYP}} = \frac{b}{1} = b\]

General Definition for ANY Angle:

If \(P\) is any point on the unit circle such that [OP] makes an angle \(\theta\) measured anticlockwise from the positive x-axis:

  • \(\cos\theta\) is the x-coordinate of P
  • \(\sin\theta\) is the y-coordinate of P

So we can write: \(P(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\)

Important Properties from the Unit Circle

Property 1: Range of Sine and Cosine

Since all points on the unit circle have coordinates \((x, y)\) where \(-1 \leq x \leq 1\) and \(-1 \leq y \leq 1\):

For any angle \(\theta\):

\[-1 \leq \cos\theta \leq 1\]

\[-1 \leq \sin\theta \leq 1\]

Property 2: The Pythagorean Identity

Since every point \((x, y)\) on the unit circle satisfies the equation \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\), and we know that \(x = \cos\theta\) and \(y = \sin\theta\):

The Pythagorean Identity:

\[\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\]

This is true for any angle \(\theta\)

Why is this useful?

This identity allows us to:

  • Find \(\sin\theta\) if we know \(\cos\theta\) (or vice versa)
  • Verify our calculations
  • Simplify trigonometric expressions

Supplementary Angles

Definition: Two angles are supplementary if their sum is \(180°\).

Example: \(60°\) and \(120°\) are supplementary because \(60° + 120° = 180°\)

When we have an angle \(\theta\) and its supplement \((180° - \theta)\), there is a special relationship between their sine and cosine values.

Supplementary Angle Relationships:

\[\cos(180° - \theta) = -\cos\theta\]

\[\sin(180° - \theta) = \sin\theta\]

Notice: The sine values are equal, but the cosine values are opposite

Review: Basic Area Formula for Triangles

You already know that the area of a triangle is:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\]

The Problem: What if we don't know the perpendicular height?

Sometimes we only know:

  • Two sides of a triangle
  • The angle between those two sides (the included angle)

In these cases, we can use trigonometry to find the area!

Triangle Labeling Convention

Before we derive the area formula, we need to establish a standard way of labeling triangles.

Convention for Labeling Triangles:

For triangle ABC:

  • The angles at vertices A, B, and C are labeled \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) respectively
  • The sides opposite these angles are labeled \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) respectively

Key Correspondences:

  • Angle \(A\) is opposite side \(a\)
  • Angle \(B\) is opposite side \(b\)
  • Angle \(C\) is opposite side \(c\)

Key Pattern: Each angle and its opposite side have the same letter!

This convention makes it easy to write formulas that work for any triangle.

Deriving the Area Formula: Acute Triangles

Let's derive a formula for the area when we know two sides and the included angle.

Case 1: Acute Triangle

Step 1: We know that Area \(= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\)

Using base = \(a\) and height = \(h\):

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ah\]

Step 2: Find \(h\) using trigonometry in the right triangle ACD:

\[\sin C = \frac{h}{b}\]

Therefore: \[h = b\sin C\]

Step 3: Substitute into the area formula:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C\]

Deriving the Area Formula: Obtuse Triangles

Case 2: Obtuse Triangle (when angle C is greater than 90°)

Step 1: Area \(= \frac{1}{2}ah\) (same as before)

Step 2: In the right triangle ACD, the angle at C is now \((180° - C)\):

\[\sin(180° - C) = \frac{h}{b}\]

Therefore: \[h = b\sin(180° - C)\]

Step 3: But we know from supplementary angles that \(\sin(180° - C) = \sin C\)

So: \[h = b\sin C\]

Step 4: Substitute into the area formula:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C\]

Amazing Result: The formula works for both acute and obtuse triangles!

Area of a Triangle Formula

Given two sides and the included angle:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C\]

or equivalently:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}bc\sin A\]

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ac\sin B\]

How to use this formula:

  1. Identify the two sides you know
  2. Identify the angle between those two sides (the included angle)
  3. Multiply: \(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{side}_1 \times \text{side}_2 \times \sin(\text{included angle})\)

The Included Angle: The angle between two sides is called the included angle.

For example, angle C is included between sides \(a\) and \(b\).

Worked Example: Finding Area

Example: Find the area of triangle ABC.

Solution:

Step 1: Identify what we know

• We know sides: \(a = 15\) cm and \(c = 11\) cm

• The angle between them is \(B = 28°\)

Step 2: Choose the correct formula

Since we have sides \(a\) and \(c\) with included angle \(B\), we use:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ac\sin B\]

Step 3: Substitute the values

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 15 \times 11 \times \sin 28°\]

Step 4: Calculate

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 15 \times 11 \times 0.46947...\]

\[\text{Area} \approx 38.7 \text{ cm}^2\]

Important: Make sure your calculator is in degree mode!

Summary

The Unit Circle

  • The unit circle has equation \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\)
  • For any angle \(\theta\): \(P(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\)
  • Range: \(-1 \leq \cos\theta \leq 1\) and \(-1 \leq \sin\theta \leq 1\)
  • Pythagorean identity: \(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\)
  • Supplementary angles:
    • \(\cos(180° - \theta) = -\cos\theta\)
    • \(\sin(180° - \theta) = \sin\theta\)

Area of a Triangle

Given two sides and the included angle:

\[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C\]

This formula works for any triangle (acute, obtuse, or right-angled)
